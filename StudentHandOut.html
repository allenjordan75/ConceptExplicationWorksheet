Meaning Analysis II — Dimensions

Choose two of the love languages. For each one, identify 2–3 dimensions that represent distinct aspects of what that love language could look like. Each dimension should capture one specific facet of the broader love language.

In McLeod’s meaning analysis, dimensions are the major facets or components of a concept. Each dimension represents one coherent way the concept can vary while still counting as the same overall thing.

Together, the dimensions map out the “territory” of the concept so that:

  • Each dimension captures a distinct aspect of the broader concept.
  • Dimensions can vary somewhat independently (e.g., high on one, lower on another).
  • They help you move from a fuzzy everyday idea to a clearer, research-ready concept.

For example, with “quality time,” you might treat attentive presence, shared activities, and conversation depth as separate dimensions that together define what quality time can look like.

Example

Love Language: Quality Time

  • Attentive Presence – Being mentally focused and free of distractions during shared time.
  • Shared Activities – Participating in mutually enjoyable tasks or routines.
  • Conversation Depth – Engaging in meaningful, personal dialogue rather than surface-level talk.

Meaning Analysis III — Indicators

First, choose one of your two love languages. For each of its dimensions, draft 2–3 observable indicators that specify who is acting, what they do, and—when relevant—how often, how long, or under what conditions the behavior occurs.

Guiding prompts
  • Actor: Is it clear who’s doing the behavior?
  • Action: Is the behavior specific and observable?
  • Threshold: Is there a clear time frame or count when needed?
  • Ambiguity: Any vague words that could be interpreted differently?
  • Fit: Does it truly reflect the intended dimension?
  • Refine: What one tweak (time, count, condition) would make it tighter?
